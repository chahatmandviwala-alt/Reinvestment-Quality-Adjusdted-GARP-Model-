qPEG

Adjusts a stock's PEG for the return it earns on capital and how durably it reinvests, then places it on a Growth-At-Reasonable-Price scale. The exponent α is derived from the reinvestment rate and a moat-based fade period, not entered by hand.

qPEG = PEG × ( h ÷ ROIC )α

About this model

The idea. A plain PEG treats all growth as equal. But growth costs capital, and ROIC tells you the price. qPEG discounts the PEG for the excess return a business earns over its hurdle: qPEG = PEG × (h/ROIC)^α. Below ~1.0 is reasonably priced; above ~1.25 is expensive.

ROIC and reinvestment. ROIC is entered directly. The reinvestment rate is computed per year as (capex + ΔWC + EBIT − EBITDA) ÷ (EBIT × (1 − tax)) and averaged over three years. EBIT − EBITDA is just minus depreciation, so the numerator is net reinvestment and the denominator is NOPAT.

The exponent α is derived, not guessed. The excess return is not held flat: over a fade period T it declines linearly from today's level to the hurdle, while the capital base compounds at RR × ROIC. The model values that fading economic profit and turns it into the exponent α applied to (h/ROIC). T is set by the moat you select, or by the three-year ROIC trend when none is chosen, and stays editable.

Fair value is the price at the midpoint of the fair band (qPEG 1.0–1.25) on the dividend-inclusive (PEGY) basis. Everything is a screening lens on the numbers you enter, not investment advice. Cyclical businesses need their inputs normalised to mid-cycle first, and three years is a thin basis for estimating a long moat.
